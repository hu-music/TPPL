Personalized Music Transfer via Compositional User Preference Transformer on Symbolic Music: Demonstrations
Figure 8: Transfer based on one piece of the user’s favorite music

Fig. 8 demonstrates the example where the transferred music is generated based on one piece of the user’s favorite music and one piece of randomly selected music. The transferred music develops its unique characteristics while effectively inheriting characteristics from the user’s favorite music and the input music. Specifically, the transferred music preserves “rising-falling contour” from the user’s favorite music, which is a unique melody feature of this music. The transferred music also preserves overlapping notes and the 3/4 time signature from the input music by containing the same music structure of each bar. As for the uniqueness, the most noticeable difference is its new melody with a different rhythm pattern, which is emphasized in orange parentheses. For example, the first four notes consist of two quarter notes and two following eighth notes, which conveys a soothing, undulating and romantic feel. The continuous appearance of this pattern reinforces this feeling, thus creating a style that is coherent and naturally different from the two source pieces. Although some melodic features contain in the new melody, the transferred music has developed a completely different “melodic outline”, giving a comfortable and smooth sense of hearing.

Figure 9: Transfer based on two pieces of the user’s favorite music.

Fig. 9 demonstrates the example where the transferred music is generated based on two pieces of the user’s favorite music and one piece of randomly selected music. The transferred music preserves “falling-rising-falling contour” from two pieces of the user’s favorite music, rhythmic patterns and the 4/4 time signature from the input music. However, characteristics from three source pieces are reorganized, fused and subtly integrated into a new melody. Specifically, the “falling-rising-falling” contours from the user’s favorite music are subtly combined into a new wave-like melodic outline, which brings different and complex auditory effects, creating emotional ups and downs.

Further analysis

Based on these examples, we see that the transferred music share similar features with their source pieces in terms of melodic contour, rhythmic pattern and time signature. However, most visible similarities show flexible changes in note-level details such as pitch and duration. In addition, the transferred music develops new features that are not present in the original music, which results in a distinct and novel feeling compared to original music.
However, the model also has limitations. If there are more than one piece of favorite music, the model tends to learn common music patterns without distinct difference. For example, in Fig. 9, two pieces of user’s favorite music contain similar ‘’falling-rising-falling” patterns though the absolute pitch value might be different. If the user’s favorite music is diverse, the model tends to “smoothing over” distinct features or learn features from one input. This is because the model tends to learn common features among all music samples and transfer the input music to fit common features. If the difference among the user’s favorite music is too large, the model tends to follow those common features while ignore differences.
